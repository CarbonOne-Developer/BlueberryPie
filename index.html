04 · The Misalignment

The two ledgers disagree.

Share of spend against share of carbon, category by category. If money and carbon tracked together, every pair would match. They do not.

The headline. Blueberries and packaging account for 57% of spend but only 37% of emissions.. The remaining categories account for just 43% of spend while driving 63% of the footprint. Reducing cost and reducing carbon are not the same job.

The trade-off. Chilean blueberries are approximately 3% cheaper than locally sourced Canadian blueberries at the ingredient level, translating into just over a 1% reduction in the cost of the finished pie. However, they increase the carbon footprint of the blueberry ingredient by 26% and total product emissions by ~2%. Transportation emissions are the primary driver of this difference, with shipments from Chile generating 4.3× more transport-related emissions. While the imported option offers modest cost savings today, it also introduces greater supply chain complexity, 4.3× greater exposure to fuel-related cost pressures and carries a higher carbon impact. Understanding these trade-offs enables sourcing teams to balance cost, carbon, and resilience before decisions are made.
10 · WHAT THE SIDE-BY-SIDE REVIEW TELLS YOU

What to take away.

01
Money and carbon do not line up
57% of spend buys 37% of carbon. Optimising the invoice will not optimise the footprint; they need separate attention.
02
The carbon-dense dollars are dairy, flour, sugar and egg
These staples carry two to three times the carbon per dollar of the blueberries and packaging that dominate the bill. That is where reduction effort belongs.
03
The cheaper import embeds risk and is the expensive carbon choice
Chilean blueberries offer modest cost savings today, but expose the organization to greater supply chain complexity, increased sensitivity to volatile fuel prices, and a 2% increase in the footprint of the finished pie. In this case, sourcing from Canadian growers represents a low-effort opportunity to reduce emissions while strengthening supply chain resilience.
